The Algorithm's Patient
Three patients. Three algorithms. Three ways bias harms the people you're supposed to protect.
You are reviewing patients on a busy afternoon. For each one, the AI has already generated an output — a risk score, a recommendation, a result. Your job is to recognize what's wrong, name the bias mechanism at work, apply an ethics framework, and decide what you do next as a DNP.

Every scenario is drawn from documented clinical AI failures in the published literature. These patients are fictional. The harm patterns are real.

📚 What This Activity Covered

You worked through three documented patterns of algorithmic bias: measurement bias (SpO2 inaccuracy in darker skin tones feeding AI tools), proxy variable bias (race used as a biological proxy in eGFR, and cost used as a proxy for need in care management), and the systemic invisibility of patients whose low utilization masks high clinical need. Each scenario required you to both identify the mechanism and apply an ethics principle — because recognizing bias without acting on it is not enough.
